IEC 61968 — Voltage Sag Detection and Recovery Procedures
=========================================================

Section 1: Scope and Applicability

This document specifies the procedures for detecting, classifying, and mitigating
voltage sag events in medium-voltage and high-voltage distribution networks, as
defined under IEC 61968 Part 9 (Interfaces for Metering and Control). Voltage sags
(also known as voltage dips per IEC 61000-4-30) are short-duration reductions in
RMS voltage magnitude, typically lasting from half a cycle (10 ms at 50 Hz) to
one minute.

Section 2: Classification of Voltage Sag Events

Voltage sags are classified by magnitude and duration according to the ITIC
(CBEMA) curve and the IEC 61000-2-8 compatibility framework:

  Type A — Shallow Sag: Voltage drops to 80-90% of nominal (0.80-0.90 pu).
           Duration: 0.5 to 30 cycles. Common cause: remote fault clearing.
           Severity: LOW. No immediate operator action required.

  Type B — Moderate Sag: Voltage drops to 50-80% of nominal (0.50-0.80 pu).
           Duration: 3 to 30 cycles. Common cause: nearby single-line-to-ground
           (SLG) fault or transformer energization inrush.
           Severity: MEDIUM. Operator should monitor and log.

  Type C — Deep Sag: Voltage drops below 50% of nominal (< 0.50 pu).
           Duration: 1 to 60 cycles. Common cause: multi-phase fault on
           adjacent feeder or bus fault.
           Severity: HIGH to CRITICAL. Immediate investigation required.

Section 3: Detection Methodology

3.1 Per-Unit Voltage Monitoring
The protection system shall continuously monitor per-unit voltage at each bus.
Normal operating range: 0.95 to 1.05 pu. A voltage sag event is declared when
the RMS voltage drops below 0.90 pu for more than 0.5 cycles.

3.2 Wavelet-Based Detection
Discrete Wavelet Transform (DWT) using Daubechies-4 (db4) mother wavelet is
recommended for transient detection. The detail coefficients at decomposition
levels 1-3 capture the high-frequency transient signature of voltage sag onset.
A threshold on the energy of level-1 detail coefficients provides reliable
sag detection with less than 2 ms latency.

3.3 Event Timestamping
All sag events must be timestamped using GPS-synchronized clocks (per IEEE
C37.118.1 synchrophasor standard) with accuracy better than 1 microsecond.

Section 4: Recovery Procedures

4.1 Automatic Voltage Regulation (AVR)
For Type A sags, the on-load tap changer (OLTC) of the supply transformer should
automatically adjust within 3-5 tap change operations to restore voltage to the
0.95-1.05 pu band. Response time: typically 30-90 seconds.

4.2 Capacitor Bank Switching
For Type B sags, automatic capacitor bank switching shall be initiated to provide
reactive power compensation. The switched capacitor banks should inject sufficient
MVAr to raise bus voltage by 0.02-0.05 pu per bank. A minimum inter-switching
interval of 300 ms must be maintained to prevent overvoltage transients.

4.3 Manual Intervention for Deep Sags
Type C sags require operator assessment. The following checklist applies:
  Step 1: Confirm sag magnitude and affected buses via SCADA display.
  Step 2: Check protection relay operation logs for upstream fault indication.
  Step 3: Verify whether automatic reclosing has restored the faulted circuit.
  Step 4: If the sag persists, check load transfer options to alternate feeders.
  Step 5: If load transfer is required, obtain dispatcher approval before
          initiating switching operations (IEC 61968 safety protocol).
  Step 6: Document the event in the disturbance log with timestamp, duration,
          magnitude, and corrective actions taken.

Section 5: Coordination with Protection Systems

Voltage sag recovery must be coordinated with overcurrent protection relay
settings (IEEE C37.112) to avoid nuisance tripping during OLTC adjustments or
capacitor switching. Time-current coordination studies should be reviewed
quarterly to ensure selectivity is maintained.

Section 6: Reporting Requirements

All voltage sag events with magnitude below 0.85 pu or duration exceeding
10 cycles must be reported in the monthly power quality report per IEC 61968
Part 9 data exchange format. The report shall include: event timestamp, bus ID,
sag magnitude (pu), sag duration (cycles), probable cause, and corrective
action taken.
