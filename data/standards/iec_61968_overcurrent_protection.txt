IEC 61968 — Overcurrent Protection Coordination Procedures
============================================================

Section 1: Scope

This document defines the standard procedures for overcurrent fault detection,
protection relay coordination, and circuit interruption in power distribution
systems per IEC 61968 (Application Integration at Electric Utilities) and
IEC 60255 (Measuring Relays and Protection Equipment). It covers both phase
overcurrent and ground fault overcurrent scenarios.

Section 2: Overcurrent Fault Types

2.1 Phase Overcurrent
Occurs when the current in one or more phase conductors exceeds the relay
pickup setting. Common causes include:
  - Short circuit between phases (phase-to-phase fault)
  - Overloaded feeder beyond thermal rating
  - Stalled motor load drawing locked-rotor current (typically 6-8x rated)

2.2 Ground Fault Overcurrent
Occurs when fault current flows through the ground return path. Detected by
residual current measurement (sum of three phase currents). Common causes:
  - Single-line-to-ground (SLG) fault due to insulation failure
  - Tree contact with overhead line
  - Cable dig-in on underground feeders

Section 3: Protection Relay Settings

3.1 Pickup Current (Threshold)
The overcurrent relay pickup shall be set at 125-150% of the maximum expected
load current for the protected feeder, per IEC 60255-151. This provides a
margin above normal load variations while ensuring reliable fault detection.

  Ipickup = 1.25 × Iload_max (minimum)
  Ipickup = 1.50 × Iload_max (recommended for feeders with high load variance)

3.2 Time-Current Characteristics
Standard inverse-time curves per IEC 60255-151:
  - Standard Inverse (SI): t = 0.14 / (I^0.02 - 1) × TMS
  - Very Inverse (VI): t = 13.5 / (I - 1) × TMS
  - Extremely Inverse (EI): t = 80.0 / (I^2 - 1) × TMS

Where I is the ratio of fault current to pickup current, and TMS is the Time
Multiplier Setting. The choice of curve depends on the downstream load
characteristics and coordination requirements.

3.3 Instantaneous Element
The instantaneous overcurrent element shall be set at 6-10x the relay pickup
current to provide fast clearance for close-in faults. Typical clearing
time: 50-80 ms (3-5 cycles at 50 Hz).

Section 4: Coordination Principles

4.1 Time Grading
Adjacent relays in the feeder chain must be coordinated with a minimum time
grading margin of 0.3-0.4 seconds (per IEC 60255) between upstream and
downstream devices. This ensures the relay closest to the fault operates
first, minimizing the extent of the outage.

4.2 Selectivity
The protection scheme must achieve selectivity: only the relay immediately
upstream of the fault should operate. If selectivity is lost (backup relay
also operates), the coordination study must be revised.

4.3 Backup Protection
Each overcurrent relay must have a backup relay at the next upstream level.
If the primary relay fails to clear the fault within its designated time plus
breaker failure time (typically 150 ms), the backup relay shall operate.

Section 5: Fault Clearance Procedure

  Step 1: Overcurrent condition detected — relay pickup threshold exceeded.
  Step 2: Relay timing element starts — operates per selected TCC curve.
  Step 3: Relay issues trip signal to circuit breaker.
  Step 4: Circuit breaker opens — fault current interrupted.
  Step 5: Automatic reclosing (if enabled) — first reclose attempt after
          dead time (typically 0.5-1.0 seconds).
  Step 6: If fault persists after reclose — relay trips again (lockout).
  Step 7: Operator notified via SCADA alarm — manual investigation required.

Section 6: Post-Fault Analysis

After any overcurrent event, the following data must be logged per IEC 61968
data exchange standards:
  - Fault current magnitude (kA) and duration (ms)
  - Relay operation time and curve used
  - Breaker operation time and number of reclose attempts
  - Affected feeder and bus identifiers
  - Probable cause (if determined)

Section 7: Testing and Maintenance

Overcurrent relay settings must be verified during commissioning and at
intervals not exceeding 4 years (per IEC 60255-27). Secondary injection
testing confirms pickup accuracy within ±5% and timing accuracy within ±5%.
